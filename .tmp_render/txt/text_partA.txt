Diarrhea and Dehydration in Children: Texts
Text A
Definition and Classification of Diarrhea in Children
Diarrhea is defined as the passage of three or more loose or watery stools within a 24-hour period. In children, 
it is one of the leading causes of morbidity and mortality worldwide, particularly in low- and middle-income 
countries, where inadequate access to healthcare and clean water amplifies its impact. The World Health 
Organization estimates that diarrheal disease contributes to nearly half a million deaths in children under five 
years of age annually.
Diarrhea in children is classified according to its duration. Acute diarrhea lasts fewer than 14 days and is the 
most commonly encountered form in clinical practice. Persistent diarrhea is defined as an episode lasting 14 
days or more but fewer than 30 days and carries an increased risk of nutritional deterioration. Chronic diarrhea 
refers to episodes extending beyond 30 days and often indicates an underlying gastrointestinal condition 
requiring further investigation.
Infectious causes predominate in paediatric populations and include viral pathogens such as rotavirus, 
norovirus,  and  adenovirus;  bacterial  organisms  including  Salmonella,  Campylobacter,  Shigella,  and 
enterotoxigenic Escherichia coli; and parasitic infections such as Giardia lamblia. Rotavirus remains the most 
common viral cause globally and accounts for a significant proportion of hospitalisations in young children.
Dehydration is the most serious complication of diarrhea and is classified as mild, moderate, or severe based 
on  clinical  signs.  Mild  dehydration  presents  with  thirst  and  minimal  clinical  findings,  while  moderate 
dehydration is characterised by sunken eyes, dry mucous membranes, and reduced skin turgor. Severe 
dehydration involves altered consciousness and signs of circulatory compromise.
Continued fluid and nutritional loss without adequate replacement may lead to hypovolemic shock and, if 
untreated, death. Electrolyte disturbances including hyponatraemia and metabolic acidosis may further 
complicate the clinical course.
Text B
Risk Factors and Prevention Strategies
Several patient-related and environmental factors increase a child's susceptibility to diarrheal illness and the 
risk of severe dehydration.
Age is a key determinant, with children under two years of age being at greatest risk due to immature immune 
function and dependence on caregivers for hygiene practices. Malnutrition impairs intestinal mucosal integrity 
and immune defences, increasing both the frequency and severity of diarrheal episodes. Zinc deficiency in 
particular compromises mucosal repair mechanisms and cellular immune responses.
Environmental risk factors include inadequate access to clean water, poor sanitation, and unhygienic food 
preparation practices. Lack of breastfeeding in infancy removes a key source of passive immunity, particularly 
secretory immunoglobulin A, and increases the child's exposure to contaminated feeds and utensils.
Preventive  strategies  aim  to  reduce  pathogen  exposure  and  strengthen  host  resistance.  Exclusive 
breastfeeding for the first six months of life is strongly recommended and significantly reduces diarrheal 
morbidity. Rotavirus vaccination has demonstrated effectiveness in reducing the incidence of severe rotavirus 
diarrhea in children and is now incorporated into national immunisation programmes in many countries. 
Promotion of hand hygiene with soap and water and provision of safe water are fundamental public health 
measures.
Oral rehydration solution (ORS) administration at the onset of diarrhea prevents progression to significant 
dehydration. Zinc supplementation given alongside ORS has been shown to reduce the duration and severity 
of acute diarrheal episodes and is recommended by the World Health Organization for all children presenting 
with acute diarrhea.

Text D
Step-by-Step Management of Diarrhea and Dehydration
Step 1: Initial clinical assessment
All children presenting with diarrhea should undergo a thorough history and physical examination, including 
assessment of stool frequency, consistency, presence of blood or mucus, vomiting, fever, and recent feeding 
history. Vital signs, body weight, and a detailed evaluation of hydration status should be documented at 
presentation.
Step 2: Classification of dehydration
Clinicians should evaluate skin turgor, mucous membrane moisture, eye appearance, level of consciousness, 
and capillary refill time to determine whether dehydration is absent, mild to moderate, or severe. In infants, 
fontanelle tension and the presence of tears provide additional diagnostic information.
Step 3: Rehydration plan
Children with no dehydration receive increased fluids at home using ORS (Plan A). Moderate dehydration 
requires supervised ORS of 75 ml per kilogram over four hours in a healthcare facility (Plan B). Severe 
dehydration requires prompt intravenous fluid resuscitation with Ringer's lactate or normal saline at 100 ml 
per kilogram (Plan C), with continuous monitoring.
Step 4: Nutritional support
Feeding should not be withheld during diarrhea, as continued feeding maintains intestinal integrity, supports 
recovery, and prevents malnutrition. Age-appropriate foods should be resumed as soon as the child can 
tolerate oral intake, and zinc supplementation is recommended for a minimum of ten days.
Step 5: Monitoring and discharge planning
Children should be monitored for resolution of dehydration, reduction in stool frequency, and return of normal 
feeding. Caregivers should be educated on recognising warning signs and when to seek further medical 
attention before the child is discharged.
