# Speaking Module

## 1. OET Speaking module in one view

The OET Speaking sub-test is profession-specific. The candidate plays the role of their own healthcare profession, such as nurse, doctor, pharmacist, physiotherapist, dentist, radiographer, etc. The test involves two interactive role-plays with a real interlocutor, and the scenarios are designed to reflect realistic healthcare communication with a patient, carer, family member, or sometimes another healthcare worker. (OET Support)

| Item | Real OET Speaking format |
| --- | --- |
| Total test time | Around 20 minutes, often longer including checks |
| Main assessed task | Two role-plays |
| Preparation time | 3 minutes before each role-play |
| Speaking time | Around 5 minutes per role-play |
| Candidate role | Candidate’s own profession |
| Other speaker | Interlocutor playing patient/carer/relative/healthcare worker |
| Assessment | Recorded and marked later by OET assessors |
| Main focus | Spoken English + patient-centred healthcare communication |

OET says each role-play is designed to produce an assessable sample of speech and usually comes to a natural end at around the 5-minute mark. Candidates can make notes on the role-play card, refer to the card during the role-play, and must return the card at the end. (OET)

The most important business point for your academy is this:

> OET Speaking is not a memorised speech test. It is a controlled healthcare role-play where the student must manage a real-time patient-style conversation.

## 2. What actually happens in the Speaking exam

There is no traditional “examiner” asking random interview questions like IELTS. OET uses an interlocutor, which means a trained person who conducts the test and plays the role of the patient, carer, relative, or other person in the scenario.

A real OET Speaking flow looks like this:

| Stage | What happens |
| --- | --- |
| Identity/profession check | Candidate’s ID and profession are confirmed |
| Warm-up conversation | Short introductory conversation to relax the candidate |
| Role-play 1 card | Candidate receives or sees the first role-play card |
| 3-minute preparation | Candidate reads the card, plans, and may ask about unclear words |
| Role-play 1 | Candidate speaks with interlocutor for about 5 minutes |
| Role-play 2 card | Candidate receives or sees the second card |
| 3-minute preparation | Candidate prepares again |
| Role-play 2 | Candidate completes second 5-minute role-play |
| Recording submitted | Audio is assessed later by official assessors |

For online delivery, OET support explains that the candidate speaks to a real person and receives a role-play card containing the scenario details. For OET on paper, the Speaking test is in person at the venue; for OET on Computer and OET@Home, Speaking is conducted online with a real interlocutor, depending on the test type and booking date. (OET Support)

For your platform, this means you should not build Speaking as only “record yourself answering questions.” You need a live role-play system, because the real test depends on interaction, listening, responding, turn-taking, empathy, and conversation control.

## 3. The interlocutor’s role

The interlocutor is not there to judge the student’s score. The interlocutor’s job is to:

| Interlocutor responsibility | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Conduct the test | Guide the candidate through the test process |
| Play the role | Act as patient, carer, relative, or healthcare worker |
| Follow role-play information | Respond according to the interlocutor card/script |
| Keep the interaction realistic | Give prompts, concerns, emotions, or resistance |
| Help the test run smoothly | Signal when the role-play should finish |
| Not mark the candidate | Assessment happens later from the recording |

OET states that the Speaking sub-test is recorded and the audio is assessed by two official OET assessors using the official Linguistic and Clinical Communication Criteria. (OET)

So teach students this clearly:

> The interlocutor is your conversation partner, not your scorer. Your job is to communicate effectively with the patient/person in front of you.

## 4. The warm-up conversation

Before the two role-plays, there is usually a short warm-up conversation. This may include questions about the candidate’s work, background, profession, or experience.

This part is not the main assessed role-play performance. It is mainly used to make the candidate comfortable and confirm the speaking interaction is working. OET’s computer test-day guidance says the interlocutor starts with a warm-up to help the candidate feel comfortable and that this part is not assessed. (OET)

For your platform, include a warm-up simulator so students stop panicking at the beginning.

Good warm-up practice questions:

| Question type | Example |
| --- | --- |
| Professional background | “Can you tell me about your role as a nurse?” |
| Workplace | “Where do you currently work?” |
| Motivation | “Why are you taking OET?” |
| Experience | “What kind of patients do you usually work with?” |
| Future plan | “Where do you hope to work after passing OET?” |

Platform feature: record the warm-up but label it as confidence practice, not scored role-play.

## 5. The role-play card

Each role-play card gives the candidate the situation and the tasks they need to cover. The card is profession-specific. For example, a nurse receives a nursing scenario, a doctor receives a medical scenario, and a pharmacist receives a pharmacy scenario.

Typical role-play card sections:

| Card section | What it contains |
| --- | --- |
| Setting | Hospital ward, GP clinic, community pharmacy, home visit |
| Candidate role | Nurse, doctor, pharmacist, physiotherapist, etc. |
| Patient/person role | Patient, parent, relative, carer, colleague |
| Background | Situation and reason for interaction |
| Tasks | What the candidate should ask, explain, advise, reassure, or arrange |
| Emotional cue | Patient may be anxious, angry, confused, reluctant |
| Clinical content | Condition, treatment, medication, procedure, follow-up |

OET says candidates have three minutes before each role-play to prepare with the card. During this time, they should understand the situation, think about the patient’s feelings, anticipate expectations, simplify medical language, and manage time. (OET)

For your platform, every role-play card should have two versions:

| Version | Purpose |
| --- | --- |
| Candidate card | What the student sees |
| Interlocutor card | What the tutor/actor sees, including emotional responses and prompts |

This is very important. Many weak OET speaking platforms only give the student card. A serious academy platform must also have the interlocutor script, otherwise tutors cannot run consistent role-plays.

## 6. The 3-minute preparation time

The 3-minute preparation time is critical. Students should not use it only to read the card once. They should actively plan the conversation.

During the 3 minutes, students should identify:

| Question | Student should decide |
| --- | --- |
| Who am I speaking to? | Patient, parent, carer, relative, colleague |
| Where are we? | Clinic, ward, discharge room, pharmacy |
| What is the purpose? | Explain, reassure, advise, gather history, persuade |
| What is the patient feeling? | Worried, angry, embarrassed, confused, resistant |
| What medical terms need simplification? | Diagnosis, procedure, medication, side effect |
| What tasks need more time? | Explanation, reassurance, shared decision-making |
| How should I start? | Greeting, role, purpose, permission |
| What should I check? | Understanding, concerns, expectations |

OET also says candidates can ask the interlocutor questions during preparation, for example about unfamiliar words on the card, but not about how to complete the task. (OET)

Platform feature: create a 3-minute prep screen with:

| Feature | Why it matters |
| --- | --- |
| Countdown timer | Builds exam realism |
| Highlighting | Student marks key tasks |
| Notes box | Student writes quick plan |
| Emotion selector | Student identifies patient mood |
| Purpose selector | Student identifies role-play goal |
| Medical-language simplifier | Student prepares lay explanations |
| “Start role-play” button | Simulates the move from prep to speaking |

## 7. The two role-plays

The candidate completes two separate role-plays. OET says having two scenarios gives candidates two opportunities to demonstrate spoken proficiency and use different types of language, giving assessors a broader view of their speaking skills. (OET)

Typical role-play situations:

| Profession | Example role-play situation |
| --- | --- |
| Nursing | Discharge advice, wound care, pain management, medication instructions |
| Medicine | Explaining diagnosis, referral, lifestyle advice, investigation results |
| Pharmacy | Explaining medication use, side effects, dosage, adherence |
| Dentistry | Explaining procedure, post-extraction care, dental hygiene |
| Physiotherapy | Exercise plan, mobility advice, recovery expectations |
| Radiography | Explaining imaging procedure, safety, preparation |
| Dietetics | Dietary advice, weight management, diabetes diet |
| Occupational Therapy | Home safety, mobility aids, daily-living support |

Your platform should tag every role-play by:

| Tag | Example |
| --- | --- |
| Profession | Nursing |
| Setting | Community clinic |
| Communication goal | Reassurance |
| Clinical topic | Asthma inhaler use |
| Patient emotion | Anxious |
| Difficulty | Intermediate |
| Criteria focus | Information giving + empathy |
| Time target | 5 minutes |

## 8. What the candidate must do during the role-play

The candidate is expected to lead the interaction like a healthcare professional. OET says the Speaking sub-test is different from general English tests because the candidate, as the healthcare professional, is expected to take control of the conversation: initiating the interaction, gathering and providing information, addressing concerns, and managing time. (OET)

A strong role-play usually includes:

| Stage | What the candidate does |
| --- | --- |
| Opening | Greets, introduces role, checks identity/context if needed |
| Purpose | Explains why the conversation is happening |
| Relationship building | Shows respect, empathy, non-judgmental attitude |
| Information gathering | Uses open questions, then focused questions |
| Patient perspective | Explores concerns, expectations, feelings |
| Explanation | Gives clear, simple, organised information |
| Checking | Checks understanding and invites questions |
| Shared plan | Explains next steps and confirms agreement |
| Closing | Summarises and ends politely |

A weak student often does the opposite: reads the card like a script, talks too much, ignores the patient’s emotion, uses medical jargon, asks several questions at once, or forgets to check understanding.

## 9. Speaking assessment criteria

OET Speaking is assessed using two broad groups of criteria:

1. Linguistic Criteria
2. Clinical Communication Criteria

### A. Linguistic Criteria

The official OET criteria list four linguistic areas:

| Criterion | What it means |
| --- | --- |
| Intelligibility | Clear pronunciation, stress, rhythm, intonation |
| Fluency | Smooth speech at suitable speed, not too many pauses |
| Appropriateness of Language | Suitable tone, register, vocabulary for context |
| Resources of Grammar and Expression | Grammar and vocabulary range/accuracy |

The official level descriptors show that high-level performance involves easily understood pronunciation, fluent speech at normal speed, appropriate register/tone/lexis, ability to explain technical matters in lay terms, and flexible grammar/vocabulary.

### B. Clinical Communication Criteria

The official OET clinical communication criteria include five areas:

| Area | Indicators |
| --- | --- |
| Relationship building | Appropriate opening, respect, non-judgmental approach, empathy |
| Understanding patient perspective | Eliciting ideas, concerns, expectations; picking up cues |
| Providing structure | Logical sequencing, signposting, organising explanations |
| Information gathering | Active listening, open-to-closed questions, clarifying, summarising |
| Information giving | Checking prior knowledge, pausing, checking understanding, discovering further needs |

OET’s official descriptor document lists these clinical communication indicators and scores them from 3 = adept use to 0 = ineffective use.

For your academy platform, every feedback form should use these categories. Do not simply tell students: “Improve fluency.” Instead, say:

> “You were fluent, but you did not explore the patient’s concern before giving advice. Work on B1/B2: eliciting concerns and picking up cues.”

That is professional OET feedback.

## 10. Scoring and results

OET reports each sub-test score on a 0–500 scale in 10-point increments, and each sub-test receives its own score rather than one overall grade. (OET)

For Speaking, your platform should show:

| Score type | Example |
| --- | --- |
| Linguistic profile | Intelligibility 5/6, Fluency 4/6, etc. |
| Clinical communication profile | Relationship building 2/3, Information giving 2/3, etc. |
| Estimated OET score | Estimated 350+ |
| Estimated grade | B estimate |
| Warning | “This is an estimate, not an official OET score.” |
| Teacher recommendation | “Work on patient perspective and signposting.” |

Be careful: your academy should not claim to give an official OET Speaking score unless you are using an authorised process. You can provide estimated readiness bands, such as:

| Readiness level | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Not ready | Major communication breakdowns |
| Developing | Can complete role-play but lacks structure/empathy |
| Borderline | Generally understandable but inconsistent |
| Exam-ready | Consistently clear, structured, patient-centred |
| Strong | Confident, flexible, natural healthcare communication |

## 11. Rules and regulations students must know

| Rule / expectation | Platform implementation |
| --- | --- |
| Two role-plays | Always practise in pairs of two for full mock mode |
| 3 minutes preparation | Add strict prep timer |
| Around 5 minutes speaking | Auto-timed role-play room |
| Candidate plays own profession | Profession selector is mandatory |
| Can refer to role card | Keep card visible during role-play |
| Can make notes | Add note box or printable notes |
| Interlocutor does not assess | Teach students not to seek approval from interlocutor |
| Recording is assessed later | Record every practice role-play |
| Must communicate, not perform a memorised script | Add interaction-based scoring |
| Must use patient-friendly language | Add jargon detector / lay explanation training |
| Must not disclose real test content | Ban leaked/recalled role-play cards |

OET’s test regulations describe OET as a high-stakes English test for healthcare professionals and emphasise that candidates should become familiar with the test construct and assessment criteria. (OET)

For your academy, use official samples for familiarisation, but build your own original role-play bank. OET provides official sample tests and graded speaking samples, but you should not build your commercial content around leaked or recalled test cards. (OET)

### C. Self-practice speaking room

Not every student can practise live every day. Build self-practice too.

| Feature | Purpose |
| --- | --- |
| AI patient simulation | Student practises anytime |
| Voice recording | Student records full response |
| Speech-to-text transcript | Allows review and annotation |
| Fluency analysis | Tracks pauses, speed, repetitions |
| Jargon checker | Finds overly technical wording |
| Empathy phrase detection | Checks whether student responds to emotion |
| Replay and compare | Student compares with model response |
| Retake option | Student repeats same role-play |

Use AI carefully. It can help students practise, but for exam readiness, include human tutor evaluation.

### D. Speaking feedback dashboard

Feedback must be criterion-based.

| Feedback area | What to show |
| --- | --- |
| Intelligibility | Pronunciation clarity, stress, rhythm, listener strain |
| Fluency | Pauses, hesitation, self-correction, speed |
| Appropriateness | Tone, register, patient-friendly wording |
| Grammar/expression | Accuracy, vocabulary range, sentence control |
| Relationship building | Greeting, empathy, respect |
| Patient perspective | Concerns, ideas, expectations |
| Structure | Logical flow, signposting, summarising |
| Information gathering | Open questions, clarification, active listening |
| Information giving | Chunking, checking understanding, simple explanations |

A good student report should sound like this:

> “Your English is mostly clear, but your clinical communication is limiting your score. You gave a long explanation before asking about the patient’s concern. Practise B1/B2 patient perspective and E2/E4 information giving.”

### F. Interlocutor training module

If you are opening an academy, your tutors must learn how to act as interlocutors consistently.

Your platform should train interlocutors to:

| Skill | Why it matters |
| --- | --- |
| Follow the patient script | Keeps practice fair and consistent |
| Avoid over-helping | Real interlocutor will not coach the candidate |
| Show realistic emotion | Gives student chance to show empathy |
| Use prompts naturally | Makes the interaction feel real |
| Keep to timing | Trains exam pacing |
| Avoid correcting during role-play | Feedback comes after |
| Record useful notes | Helps teacher feedback |

This is a major business advantage: your platform can standardise your tutors.

## 14. Student analytics dashboard

| Metric | Example feedback |

| Weakest area | Patient perspective |

| Repeated issue | Does not check understanding |

A strong student report should say:

> “You are understandable and mostly fluent, but your role-play sounds too one-sided. You need to ask more open questions, respond to patient cues, and check understanding after giving information.”

This is the kind of detailed feedback students cannot get from ordinary YouTube videos.

## 16. Speaking course pathway

Do not start students with full role-plays only. Build a progressive pathway.

| Stage | Module |
| --- | --- |
| 1 | Introduction to OET Speaking format |
| 2 | Understanding the role-play card |
| 3 | Using the 3-minute preparation time |
| 4 | Opening the conversation naturally |
| 5 | Building rapport and empathy |
| 6 | Asking open and closed questions |
| 7 | Exploring ideas, concerns, and expectations |
| 8 | Explaining medical information simply |
| 9 | Signposting and organising explanations |
| 10 | Checking understanding |
| 11 | Handling angry/anxious patients |
| 12 | Managing time in 5 minutes |
| 13 | Profession-specific role-play practice |
| 14 | Recorded mock test 1 |
| 15 | Feedback and targeted drills |
| 16 | Final two-role-play exam simulation |

OET itself highlights the importance of reviewing real samples with grades, assessor feedback, and transcripts to understand what successful performance looks like. (OET)

## 17. Skill drills your platform should include

Speaking improvement requires micro-practice, not only full role-plays.

| Drill type | Example |
| --- | --- |
| Opening drills | Start the interaction appropriately |
| Empathy drills | Respond to fear, anger, embarrassment |
| ICE drills | Ask about Ideas, Concerns, Expectations |
| Open-question drills | “Can you tell me more about…?” |
| Lay-language drills | Explain “hypertension” simply |
| Signposting drills | “First, I’ll explain…, then we’ll discuss…” |
| Checking-understanding drills | “Could you tell me how you’ll take this medicine?” |
| Reassurance drills | Reassure without giving false certainty |
| Closing drills | Summarise plan and invite final questions |
| Pronunciation drills | Medical words, stress, clarity |
| Fluency drills | Reduce long pauses and fillers |
| Grammar drills | Conditional advice, modal verbs, polite language |

Example platform exercise:

> Student hears: “I’m scared this operation might go wrong.” Student must record a response showing empathy, reassurance, and next step. Platform/tutor scores it under relationship building and patient perspective.

## 20. Compliance and quality control

Because Speaking involves test-like scenarios, recordings, and possibly student personal data, your platform should include compliance features.

| Area | Platform rule |
| --- | --- |
| Student consent | Get consent before recording sessions |
| Data privacy | Store recordings securely |
| Access control | Only student, assigned tutor, and admin can access recordings |
| Content integrity | Do not use leaked real OET cards |
| Tutor standards | Train tutors to use OET criteria consistently |
| Score disclaimer | Estimated scores are not official OET scores |
| Recording retention | Let students/admin manage how long recordings are stored |
| Academic honesty | Ban memorised “real exam” content sharing |

Also include a clear warning:

> “This platform provides OET-style training and estimated feedback. Official OET scores are issued only by OET.”

> “At 02:14, the patient expressed concern, but you moved directly to advice without acknowledging the feeling.”

2. Candidate and interlocutor cards
3. Live speaking room
4. AI/self-practice room
5. 3-minute prep timer
6. 5-minute role-play timer
7. Audio/video recording

9. OET criteria-based feedback

The product should answer these questions for every student:

> “Was I clear?” “Was I fluent?” “Did I sound professional?” “Did I build rapport?” “Did I understand the patient’s concern?” “Did I structure the conversation?” “Did I check understanding?” “Am I ready for the real OET Speaking test?”

## 23. The most important business advice

For OET Speaking, your academy should not sell only “mock interviews.” The real value is a complete speaking communication system.

Your competitive advantage should be:

1. Live role-play simulation
2. Profession-specific cards
3. Trained interlocutors
4. Recording and transcript review
5. Feedback mapped to OET criteria
6. Micro-drills for empathy, structure, and information giving
7. Tutor calibration and quality control
8. AI practice for daily repetition
9. Student progress dashboard
10. Exam-readiness reports

A strong Speaking report should look like this:

> “Your estimated score is around 330–350. You are understandable and your fluency is acceptable, but your clinical communication is inconsistent. In both role-plays, you gave information before exploring the patient’s concerns. Practise open questions, empathy responses, and checking understanding before attempting Mock Speaking Set 04.”

That is the kind of feedback students will value because it tells them exactly what to improve, not just whether they “speak well.”
